FACT: Smoking kills!



Introduction

Tobacco use is a worldwide problem. Millions of people die every year of tobacco use, even people who know how bad it is for your health.

Some facts

  • The tobacco industry is a industry who depends on the poorest people in the world. Supply and demandin the tobacco industry are dependent on these people. Of the estimated one billion smokers in the world, 80 percent live in low- and middle-income countries.
  • Estimated there are around 6.5 trillion cigarettes sold around the the world each year (18 billion cigarets a day).
  • 80 percent of the one billion smokers in the worldare low-and middle- income countries.
  • Smoking awareness is the lowest in China, India and Brazil. The odd thing about this, is that these countries are the three largest tobacco-producing countries in the world.
  • Half of all smokers will die by tobacco
  • Tobacco causes six million deaths per year
  • Per cigarette someone smokes their life will reduce by five to eleven minutes. In a lifetime this can be as much as twelve years. Every cigarette you smoke cuts five to 11 minutes from your life. Over a lifetime, that can reduce your life expectancy by as much as twelve years.
  • Around 25 percent of all heart disease deaths and 75 percent of lung disease deaths are directly attributed to smoking irrespective of any other cause (Quoted from Verywell mind, 2018)".

Even with all this information about smoking and health problems people keep smoking. Why is that the case? Is quitting really so difficult for people? Is the habit so strong that people are willing to die for it?

Habits are formed over the years and how longer you do them, how harder it is to quit with them. So this website is designed to give people between the age of 14 and 30 living in The Netherlands an interactive insight in some statistics about smoking.





Description visualisations

The visualizations below show a world map, scatter plot and a line chart. In the summary below each visualisation will be described and the interface will be explained. Hoovering over each datapoint/country in each visualisation will provide a tooltip with the right information for each visualisation.

  • Worldmap: The worldmap shows data about the share of people who smoke everyday from 1990 till 2010. With the slider underneath the map you can choose the year which will be showed in the map by colors. The color gradient is based on the minimum and the maximum of the dataset. The color scale for the values of the colors is displayed next to the map.

    The worldmap is also linked to the line graph. The user can click on a country, which data then will be displayed in the linegraph in comparison with The Netherlands.

  • Scatterplot: The scatterplot will show effects of the number of cigarets consumed per smoker (x axis) on the number of deaths from tobacco smoking or the share of cancer deaths attributed to tobacco in % (y axis). The variable of the number of deaths from tobacco smoking has a logarithmic scale, so the values are going from 10 till 1.400.000 which is displayed by 1e+6 (so 1 * 10^6). The variable of the share of cancer deaths attributed to tobacco has a linear scale. The two variables can be switched by choosing a variable on the Y axis button.

    The years of the data can also be adjusted with the Year button. The years between 1990 till 2010 with steps of 5 years can be selected for the scatterplot.

    Each datapoint in the scatterplot represents a country which are also shown in de worldmap. The scatterplot is, like the worldmap, linked to the line graph. The user can click on a country. This country will be shown in the linegraph in comparison with The Netherlands.

  • Lingraph: The linegraph shows the same data as the worldmap, share of people who smoke everyday, but then from 1980 till 2012. In this way there is a global overview of the decrease in the number of smokers per clicked country in comparison with The Netherlands.

Text bargraph

Since january 1 2014 the age for buying cigarets went from 16 to 18 years old in The Netherlands. In 2016 the lay-out of a package of cigarets changed, like described in the following article:

A tongue with a lump, a dead person in a body bag, a lung affected by cancer, a woman who coughs up blood. From tomorrow tobacco manufacturers have to put this kind of frightening images on packets of cigarettes and shag. The packages as we know them now, with only a warning in text, may then no longer be produced. The purpose of the new European rules is to discourage smoking further. It will take some time before the new packages can also be seen in the stores. Retailers and producers have a year to sell their stock of old parcels (Translated from NOS, 2016).

Does these measures to discourage smoking actually help? Judge yourself.
